Who are you here for?

You noticed something. That care matters.

You don't need a label, a diagnosis, or a crisis to help someone feel less alone.

Most people who are struggling hide it — not because they can't see it, but because they're afraid of being judged. The most powerful thing you can offer isn't a fix. It's staying close.

How to be a steady presence

You can't make someone ready. You can make sure they're not alone when they are.

Ask, don't lecture

"How are you, really?" opens more doors than "you need to stop." Curiosity feels like care; pressure feels like judgment.

Normalize, don't shame

"A lot of people go through this" lands better than an ultimatum. Shame drives it underground; acceptance brings it into the light.

Stay connected, drop the timeline

Readiness comes in windows, not on schedule. Your job isn't a confession today — it's being someone they trust when a window opens.

"They don't think they have a problem."

That's the most common place to start — and it isn't only denial. The old story said you're either "a junkie" or you're fine. Nobody wants the first label, so people quietly decide they must be the second.

The fears that keep people silent

"I'll lose my job." "My family will leave." "Everyone will know." So they say nothing, sometimes for years.

What people who got support actually report: relationships got stronger. Work didn't fall apart. Quality of life went up, not down. The secret turned out to be heavier than the help.

Progress beats perfection. No one has to hit bottom — or quit everything overnight — to start feeling better.
